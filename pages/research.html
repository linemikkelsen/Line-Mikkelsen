Research
My work includes traditional syntactic and semantic research as well as applied linguistics. Both strands are highly collaborative.

My early syntactic research was on Germanic syntax with empirical focus on clause structure (verb-second), anaphora, noun-incorporation, definiteness, DP-internal syntax, copular clauses, resumption, and the syntax-pragmatics interface. Since 2010 my research has expanded to include the syntax of Karuk (Hokan, California) and Kalaallisut (Inuit, Greenland). In Karuk I have pursued my interest in resumption further and Kalaallisut has renewed my interest in copular clauses and also allowed me to work on cross-clauses interactions, such as hyperraising and prolepsis.

My applied work has grown out of my engagement with Karuk language and communities and centers on language documentation and revitalization. Specific revitalization activities include participation in language learning activities, curriculum development, development of an online dictionary and text corpus, and logistical and financial support for community activities. More recently, it has expanded to working with Chochenyo language and culture revitalization.

The work with Karuk communities also includes youth-oriented activities more broadly, annual UC Berkeley campus visits, opportunities for networking with UC Berkeley native students, and in-class presentations that contextualize the linguistic material.
